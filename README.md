# Food Insecurity in the United States
### A Congressional Brief

**47.4 million Americans live in food-insecure households today.**
**One in five American children.**

This brief makes the case for federal action on food insecurity. It draws on six federal data sources and three peer-reviewed economic studies to answer the questions that matter to the appropriations process: how widespread is hunger in America, who bears its weight, what does it cost the country, and what works to fix it.

The evidence is unambiguous. The case for action is fiscal as well as moral.

---

## The Scale

In 2024, food insecurity in the United States affected 47.4 million people — including roughly one in five children. The relationship between poverty and hunger is direct: at the state level, poverty rates and food insecurity rates correlate at 0.75. Where poverty is high, hunger follows.

The federal poverty line is the dividing line. Below it, **39 percent** of households are food insecure. Above 185 percent of poverty — the same threshold the federal government uses for WIC and reduced-price school meals — only **8 percent** are. **Food insecurity drops fivefold** when households cross out of program-eligible income brackets. The federal nutrition system was designed for these populations, and the data shows precisely where the system holds and where it strains.

---

## Who is Affected

The national rate of 13.7 percent obscures profound disparities.

- **1 in 3** female-headed households are food insecure (36.8%)
- **1 in 4** Black households are food insecure (24.4%)
- **1 in 5** Hispanic households are food insecure (20.2%)
- **1 in 5** households with children are food insecure (18.4%)

By contrast, white households without children experience food insecurity at half the national rate. Food insecurity in America is not evenly distributed. It is concentrated in the households that are also most economically vulnerable — single mothers, households of color, and families with children.

---

## We Have Proof of What Works

In 2021 — in the middle of a global pandemic — food insecurity in the United States dropped to **10.2 percent**, the lowest rate ever recorded since federal tracking began in 1995.

This was not an accident. It was the direct result of expanded federal action: the Child Tax Credit expansion sending monthly payments to families with children, SNAP emergency allotments raising every household to the maximum benefit, P-EBT replacing school meals when buildings closed, three rounds of stimulus, and universal free school meals nationwide.

When those programs expired — the Child Tax Credit expansion in December 2021, SNAP emergency allotments in March 2023, universal school meals after the 2021–22 school year — food insecurity climbed **3.5 percentage points** by 2024. Roughly 13 million additional Americans became food insecure.

We have proof of what works. We chose to stop doing it.

---

## The Cost of Inaction

**Healthcare burden.** Food-insecure adults face measurably higher rates of obesity, hypertension, and diabetes. Berkowitz et al. (2018), publishing in *Health Affairs*, calculated that food insecurity drives **$52.9 billion per year** in additional U.S. healthcare costs — borne by Medicare, Medicaid, and private insurance. Every year of inaction is a year of avoidable healthcare spending.

**Lost adult potential.** A landmark 2016 study in the *American Economic Review* tracked children who received food assistance during the rollout of the Food Stamp Program in the 1960s and 70s. Compared with children in neighboring counties who didn't receive it, those children grew into adults with:

- 27% lower rates of metabolic syndrome
- 22% lower rates of heart disease
- 21% lower rates of obesity
- 16% lower rates of high blood pressure
- 29% higher economic self-sufficiency (women)
- 13% lower reliance on adult transfer programs

A 2020 update by the same research team — using Social Security earnings records on 17 million Americans — calculated a marginal value of public funds of **56 to 1**. For every $1 the federal government invested in feeding hungry children, society received approximately $56 in social benefit through healthier adults, higher lifetime earnings, lower disability claims, and longer lives.

This is the highest-return social investment ever measured in the U.S. economy.

---

## The Fix

Eight states have already acted. As of the 2024–25 school year, California, Maine, Colorado, Minnesota, New Mexico, Vermont, Massachusetts, and Michigan provide universal free school meals to every public school student.

The measured outcomes from those states represent the largest natural experiment in U.S. nutrition policy currently in progress:

- 6% increase in school lunch participation
- 233,656 additional meals served daily
- 5.1% reduction in food insecurity among households with children
- 0.6 percentage-point reduction in childhood obesity (California CEP schools)
- 0.7 percentage-point reduction in school suspensions (Oregon)

The cost is approximately $700 per child per year. Healthcare savings alone return roughly $2.60 per dollar invested. Lifetime social return reaches $56 per dollar invested.

**Eight states have acted. Forty-two are waiting.**

---

## The Ask

Federal action on four fronts would extend the proven results from these states to the entire country:

1. **Universal free school meals nationwide.** Restore the policy that operated under pandemic-era extensions in 2020–22.
2. **Restore the expanded Child Tax Credit**, with monthly payment delivery to families below the SNAP eligibility threshold.
3. **Make permanent the SNAP emergency allotments** that expired in March 2023.
4. **Fully fund WIC** — full federal participation funding has not been achieved in any year since 2010.

The cost per child is small. The lifetime return is enormous. The evidence base is decades deep and methodologically airtight.

This is not a partisan question. It is an arithmetic one.
